The AI Speed Paradox

Right before my transition, a mantra began echoing across the organization: "Move at the speed of AI." Each time I heard it, something in me tightened; because the truth is, we're not machines. We're human. And sometimes the most radical move you can make is to slow down.

I didn't realize how addicted I'd become to being productive until I stopped. The first few weeks were jarring. I didn't miss the meetings or inbox chaos. I missed the validation. I had no sprint to run, no one to impress, and no reason to polish executive updates or PowerPoint decks at 11:58 PM. Without that drumbeat of urgency, I felt lost, floaty, and untethered.

That was the withdrawal.

We are not machines. We're not built for infinite acceleration. And pretending we are, while catchy, is the kind of logic that burns out great people under the banner of innovation.

Rediscovery

The first few weeks without structure felt like withdrawal. But once I stopped trying to fill every moment with productivity, something unexpected happened: I got curious again. Real curiosity‚Äînot the kind that's optimized for outcomes, but the kind that follows threads just to see where they lead.

I started building. Not because I had to, but because I wanted to. A portfolio site became an excuse to learn React. Learning React became an excuse to understand TypeScript. Understanding TypeScript became a gateway to exploring AI tools that could accelerate my workflow without accelerating my life.

Somewhere between debugging CSS and choosing font weights, I realized I wasn't just building a portfolio. I was rebuilding myself.

What Matters Now

This isn't a productivity hack or a framework for optimization. It's permission to move at your own speed‚Äîthe speed of what actually matters to you.

Maybe that's faster than you think. Maybe it's slower. Maybe it changes depending on the season, the project, or the day. The point isn't the pace; it's the intention behind it.

I'm still building, still learning, still figuring it out. But now I'm doing it on my terms, at my speed, guided by curiosity rather than urgency.

And that, it turns out, makes all the difference.
